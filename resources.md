# Ngoni — Resources, Provenance & Cultural Context

**Instrument:** Ngoni (Kamele N'goni)  
**Date:** 2026-05-08

## Cultural Context and Provenance

The ngoni is a plucked-string lute of the Mande peoples of West Africa, centered historically in present-day Mali, Guinea, Senegal, Burkina Faso, and The Gambia. It is one of the oldest known plucked string instruments in the West African musical tradition.

**Two principal variants exist:**

- **Donso ngoni** ("hunter's ngoni"): the traditional 6-string form, historically played by the Donso hunting brotherhoods. It carries a ceremonial and community role tied to the donso society. Its music and the role of its players are culturally specific and not casual — the donso are a structured social institution.

- **Kamele ngoni** ("youth ngoni"): a modernized variant popularized in the 1950s–1960s, credited largely to Allata Brulaye Sidibé of Guinea. The Kamele ngoni expanded the string count to 10–12 strings and moved into urban popular music contexts. It is the form most commonly built and played today outside West Africa.

The ngoni belongs to a family of West African bowl-lutes that includes the **kora** (21-string harp-lute, Mande/Mandé), the **xalam** (Senegal/Wolof), and the **akonting** (Gambia/Jola — considered a possible ancestor of the American banjo via the transatlantic slave trade). The word "ngoni" itself is thought to be one etymological ancestor of the word "banjo."

**A note on cultural respect:**  
This project documents the ngoni as an engineering object and an acoustic instrument, and approaches it with genuine admiration for its musical tradition. The instrument's origins and ongoing cultural significance to Mande griot musicians and donso hunters are acknowledged explicitly. The Donso ngoni in particular is embedded in a living social institution; builders from outside this tradition should approach its ceremonial forms with appropriate awareness. This project does not claim cultural authority over the instrument's musical tradition.
